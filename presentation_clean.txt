Presentation Draft: DeepHepaStrat / HepSense
--------------------------------------------------

1. Introduction

• Overview of the topic:
  - "HepSense" (DeepHepaStrat) is an AI-driven risk stratification system.
  - Designed exclusively to forecast acute decompensation events in patients with liver cirrhosis.
  - Shifts the clinical paradigm from static "disease staging" to dynamic, longitudinal future prediction.

• Importance of the topic:
  - Liver cirrhosis is the 11th leading cause of death globally (over 1.3 million deaths annually).
  - Current clinicians rely on outdated, static scoring equations (e.g., MELD-Na, FIB-4).
  - Static scores analyze a single day's blood test, creating a dangerous "snapshot" bias that misses declining trajectories.
  - As a result, doctors are often blind to impending, fatal crises like variceal hemorrhage or encephalopathy.
  - Implementing a temporal AI "early warning radar" is critical for reducing preventable ICU mortality.

3. Key Studies and Findings

• Kumar et al. (IEEE JBHI, 2024): This study utilized Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks on continuous electronic health records (EHR). Finding: They proved that algorithms assessing a 14-day trajectory of lab results significantly outperformed static 24-hour models at forecasting gastrointestinal bleeding.
• Zhang et al. (IEEE Access, 2023): Leveraged the massive MIMIC-IV database to analyze patients with severe liver disease. Finding: Identified that an XGBoost algorithm combining routine labs (INR, bilirubin, platelets) could predict short-term ICU mortality with an impressive 0.86 Area Under the Curve (AUC).
• Martinez et al. (IEEE ICHI, 2023): Focused exclusively on the "black box" problem in medical AI. Finding: Demonstrated that using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values to visualize feature importance increased physician trust and adoption of algorithm-based prognostics by over 60%.
• Chen et al. (IEEE TMI, 2024): Explored multimodal models (combining tabular blood data with CT scans). Finding: Confirmed that automated skeletal muscle index (SMI) extraction via Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) is a statistically significant independent predictor of cirrhosis mortality.


4. Trends in Literature

• Shift from Static to Dynamic Modeling: A rapid movement away from fixed clinical equations (MELD) toward algorithms that process sequential, time-series data to capture the velocity of a patient's decline.
• Rise of Interpretable AI (XAI): Medical journals are increasing their strictness regarding model transparency. Papers that do not feature "local explanations" (like SHAP) are frequently rejected, cementing XAI as the industry standard.
• Integration of Unstructured NLP: Researchers are finding that critical predictive signals exist in the unstructured text of doctors' notes, prompting the fusion of Natural Language Processing (NLP) with standard numeric lab data.


5. Theoretical Framework

• Explainable Temporal Machine Learning Theory: This framework posits that physiological collapse in chronic end-stage liver disease is not sudden; rather, it is preceded by subtle, long-term degradation across multiple routine biomarkers. By combining supervised time-series forecasting with local interpretability mathematical models (SHAP), the system fundamentally translates complex physiological trajectories into actionable, transparent clinical alerts.


6. Relevance to Current Research

• Most existing open-source ML medical research focuses on predicting mortality *after* a patient enters critical condition in the ICU. Our project bridges a critical clinical gap: using longitudinal data to predict the actual future onset of an emergency (decompensation) 7 to 14 days before it happens, allowing for preventative intervention rather than reactive resuscitation.


7. Problem Definition

• Clear statement of the problem: Current clinical hepatology scoring systems are purely static and mathematically fail to predict impending, acute decompensation events in cirrhotic patients.
• Context of the Problem: Intensive Care Unit (ICU) and hepatology wards generate massive amounts of data daily. Doctors suffer from "data overload" and physically lack the computational tools to track the velocity and converging trajectories of a dozen daily lab results simultaneously.
• Significance of Addressing the Problem: If this temporal data gap is not addressed, catastrophic events (such as variceal bleeding or coma) continue to occur unpredicted. This leads to massive emergency room financial burdens, prolonged ICU stays, and highly preventable patient mortality.


8. Objectives

• Main Objective: To develop and validate "HepSense," a multi-modal, temporal machine learning framework that accurately predicts specific decompensation events using longitudinal clinical data.
• Specific Objectives:
  1. Extract, clean, and chronologically align 14-day time-series EHR data from the MIT MIMIC-IV database.
  2. Implement Natural Language Processing (NLP) to extract unstructured symptom features from clinical admission notes.
  3. Construct an interactive, explainable clinical dashboard powered by SHAP values for absolute medical transparency.
• Expected Contributions: A highly novel, publishable predictive model that adds significant value to existing bioinformatics literature by providing actionable, time-sensitive forecasts to gastroenterologists rather than generic disease tracking.


9. Methodology

• Design: 
  - A quantitative, retrospective data-mining structure utilizing multi-modal clinical histories.
  - Combines advanced supervised deep learning with Natural Language Processing (NLP).
  - Shifts the diagnostic focus from single-day static datasets to dynamic 14-day temporal tracking.

• Data Collection Methods: 
  - Executing authorized, secure extraction of de-identified critical care records directly from MIT's PhysioNet repository.
  - Explicitly utilizing 4 core tables via SQL querying from the massive MIMIC-IV database:
    1. 'mimic-hosp' for tracking daily unstructured blood tests (Bilirubin, Platelets, INR).
    2. 'mimic-icu' for monitoring critical vital sign deviations (Heart Rate, GCS Scores).
    3. 'mimic-ed' for isolating the exact target timestamps when acute decompensation crises occurred in the emergency ward.
    4. 'mimic-note' for ingesting free-form textual physician assessments.

• Data Analysis Techniques: 
  - Data Preprocessing: Performing complex tabular merges via Pandas, executing missing-value imputation (using "Last Observation Carried Forward"), and chronologically formatting 14-day longitudinal lookback matrices.
  - NLP Text Extraction: Utilizing HuggingFace transformers and spaCy logic to programmatically scan raw doctor notes for critical deterioration keywords (e.g., "vomiting blood", "severe confusion").
  - Predictive Modeling: Training, hyperparameter tuning via GridSearchCV, and cross-validating Time-Series Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBoost) against a held-out testing cohort.
  - Model Interpretation: Embedding the SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) mathematical framework to transparently visualize exact feature contributions directly to the evaluating clinician.

• Tools and Resources Used: 
  - Data Base & Engineering: Python, Pandas, NumPy, and intensive SQL parsing.
  - Machine Learning & XAI: Scikit-Learn, LightGBM/XGBoost, and the SHAP Library.
  - Clinical Deployment Pipeline: Flask functionality mapped to a localized interactive dashboard interface.

  - Delivery of an interactive, perfectly interpretable clinical software dashboard for localized testing.
